\section{Vectors and Linear Combinations}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Linear combination:} Forms like \(3\bm{v}+5\bm{w}\), \(\bm{v}\) and \(\bm{w}\) are vectors.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Why some equations fail}

For example $\begin{smallmatrix} c+2d=1\\ c+3d=0\\ c+4d=0 \end{smallmatrix}$.
It's actually a linear combination $
  c\begin{bsmallmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{bsmallmatrix}
  +d\begin{bsmallmatrix} 2 \\ 3 \\ 4 \end{bsmallmatrix}
  =\begin{bsmallmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bsmallmatrix}
$,
because $\begin{bsmallmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bsmallmatrix}$ is not on that plane.

\section{Lengths and Dot Products}\
\begin{definition}[Vectors dot procuct (aka. inner product)]\leavevmode
  \label{def:vector dot_product}
  \[\bm{v}\cdot\bm{w}=v_1 w_1+v_2 w_2\]
  \begin{note} The dot product of vectors is commutative, meaning the order does not matter: $\bm{v}\cdot\bm{w} = \bm{w}\cdot\bm{v}$ \end{note}
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[vector's length]
  \label{def:vector_length}
  $\norm{v}=\sqrt{\bm{v}\cdot\bm{v}}=(v_1^2+v_2^2+\cdots+v_n^2)^{\frac{1}{2}}$\end{definition}
\subsection{Pythagoras theorem for a 3D Vector}

To find the length of a 3D vector, we apply the Pythagorean theorem twice. For example, consider the vector
\((1,2,3)\). First, calculate the length of its projection onto the \(xy\)-plane, which is \((1,2,0)\). By the
Pythagorean theorem, the length of this base is \(\sqrt{1^2 + 2^2} = \sqrt{5}\).

Next, because this base vector is perpendicular to the vertical component \((0,0,3)\), you can apply the theorem a
second time. The total length of the vector, representing the diagonal of the box, is therefore \(\norm{\bm{v}} =
\sqrt{(\sqrt{5})^2 + 3^2} = \sqrt{5+9} = \sqrt{14}\).

\subsection{The Angle Between Two Vectors}
\phantom{}
\begin{proof}[The dot product is $\bm{v}\cdot\bm{w}=0$ when $\bm{v}$ is perpendicular to $\bm{w}$.]
  When $\bm{v}$ and $\bm{w}$ are perpendicular, they form the legs of a right triangle. The third side is $\pm(\bm{v}-\bm{w})$. Apply \textit{Pythagoras Law} gets:
  \begin{equation} \label{eq:1} \norm{\bm{v}}^2+\norm{\bm{w}}^2=\norm{\bm{v}-\bm{w}}^2 \end{equation}
  Apply Definition \ref{def:vector_length} to the RHS gets
  \[\norm{\bm{v}}^2-\bm{v}\cdot\bm{w}-\bm{w}\cdot\bm{v}+\norm{\bm{w}}^2\]
  As in Definition \ref{def:vector dot_product}, vectors dot product is commutative, so the RHS could be simplified to:
  \begin{equation}\label{eq:2}\norm{\bm{v}}^2-2(\bm{v}\cdot\bm{w})+\norm{\bm{w}}^2\end{equation}
  substitute the expanded form (\ref{eq:2}) back into the (\ref{eq:1}):
  \begin{DispWithArrows*}[format=lrl]
         & \norm{\bm{v}}^2 + \norm{\bm{w}}^2 & {}= \norm{\bm{v}}^2 - 2(\bm{v}\cdot\bm{w}) + \norm{\bm{w}}^2
    \Arrow{cancel $\norm{\bm{v}}^2$ and $\norm{\bm{w}}^2$}                                                  \\
    \iff & 0                                 & {}= -2(\bm{v}\cdot\bm{w}) \Arrow{$\div -2$}                  \\
    \iff & \bm{v}\cdot\bm{w}                 & {}= 0
  \end{DispWithArrows*}
  \begin{figure}[H]
    \incfig{chapter_01_1.2}
    \caption{Perpendicular vectors}
  \end{figure}
\end{proof}
